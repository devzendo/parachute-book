\part{Preface}
\section{About this book}
    \newthought{Parachute is...} an open, simple and useful personal computing stack — built around the Inmos Transputer (in emulation).\footnote{Hold on. I want to set some expectations here - although I've been working on this for many years, off and on, this system is currently still \textit{a beginning}.} \\
\vspace{5mm}
\vspace{5mm}
        
	It is a long-term project to build a small systems architecture
        from the ground up: emulator, toolchain, OS kernel, and applications — designed
        to run on current operating systems, and cheap, widely available hardware.

\vspace{5mm}
    Small enough that a person can understand the whole system.
\vspace{5mm}

	This book is a tutorial introduction and complete reference to \textit{part} of the Parachute system: its emulator, hardware\footnote{Initially, the Raspberry Pi Pico, currently around £3.80 in the UK, July 2026.} and languages. The focus of this volume is on running single, small - but parallel - applications on it directly, with \textit{no operating system}.\footnote{Hence the 'Bare Metal' (or rather, silicon) of the title of this first volume.}
	
	In its heyday, the Transputer was used either as a network of many microcontrollers in an embedded system, or as a co-processor network for an existing mainstream computer. \footnote{The B008 PC ISA board for example.} Later developments created operating systems for pure Transputer hardware.\footnote{The Meiko Computing Surface, and the Helios operating system, for example.} 
	
	This volume focuses on the first two applications, using the Parachute emulator instead of actual Transputer hardware.\footnote{Which appears occasionally on auction sites, but is harder to work with, and is generally considered by this author to be made of \textit{Unobtanium}.}
	
	Volume two - not yet started - will cover the forthcoming microkernel OS of the Parachute system.

\section{Who should read this book}

Those who yearn for a return to simple, distraction-free devices, that won't become obsolete, due to them being buildable from open technology.

Developers and tinkerers who remember when personal computing felt like a frontier — or who wish they'd been there.

People who want to understand their tools - all the way down.

Anyone who thinks the direction of travel in modern systems (more abstraction, more complexity, more magic, less freedom, no right-to-repair) isn't the only possible direction...

Perhaps innovative ideas from the past were before their time, and had much to offer - and are worth re-visiting in a modern setting.

Those who would like to help form a community building this: it's intended to be a community project as much as a technical one. \newthought{There's a lot to build. \textit{Come help}.}


\section{How to read this book}
The book can be read sequentially - each section builds on the previous one.

The next part, Foundations on page \pageref{foundations}, describes the vision and motivation behind the project. 

It then gives a high-level description of the Transputer processor, and how our emulator relates to this. There are many differences.\footnote{Appendix \ref{appendix:compatibility} on page \pageref{appendix:compatibility} details how accurately the emulator mirrors the behaviour of real Transputers}.

When used as a co-processor to a host computer, the Transputer made use of host computer I/O facilities - access the filesystem, the real-time clock, the textual console and keyboard. This client/server aspect of Transputers is explained in depth, which leads into a glimpse forward to the device support aspect of the forthcoming OS.\\

\vfill

After gaining a solid understanding of the architecture provided by Parachute, the next part of the book, Getting Started on page \pageref{getting-started} covers downloading, verifying, and installing the software for the various supported platforms, and also flashing the emulator image onto Raspberry Pi Pico boards.

Once installed, the Programming section of the book on page \pageref{tutorial-introduction} gives a variety of small programs, in several languages, that will show you more of the architecture, and how to work effectively with the 'bare metal' emulator.

These examples grow in complexity, and move from single processes, to multiple co-operating processes, to multiple processors in networks.

\vfill

Finally the book ends with a complete reference on page \pageref{reference} to the various programs and languages available within the system.

\vfill

By the end of the book, you should be able to write small programs for this interesting modern 'take' on an architecture that was once poised to change the world.

\newthought{The future was parallel. \textit{Explore it}.}

\subsection{Policy on contributions and use of Large Language Models ('AI')}
I welcome contributions from humans via pull requests on all aspects of the system. \\
\vspace{5mm}
I use Claude.ai occasionally for \textit{small-scale} assistance with code, prose and advice, and as a more focused search/analysis tool. \textbf{All} its output is vetted thoroughly by me. I am \textit{highly alert} to its potential for hallucination and its propensity to confabulate and mislead. \\
\vspace{5mm}
I gratefully acknowledge the work of others that was stolen without consent in the creation of Large Language Models. I understand the environmental impact of this technology. \\
\vspace{5mm}

The largest use of Claude.ai in this project has been the translation of the Small-C compiler and assembler \footnote{Written by Óscar Toledo Gutiérrez} from Spanish to English, which has been tested thoroughly by me. \\
\vspace{5mm}

While I have experimented in other projects with \textit{vibe coding}, this project does \textbf{not} use it. I only use it for creating examples of technologies I need to get up to speed with rapidly - and these are vetted, and treated with suspicion. \textit{As they should be - LLMs are rightly 'Artificial' Intelligence.} Real intelligence is an asymptote that LLMs can never reach. \\
\vspace{5mm}
Should you wish to submit changes or pull requests to any of the Parachute projects, I \textit{strongly prefer} Test-Driven Development - i.e. where pragmatic, tests are \textit{required}. And actual TDD - not 'test-after'. \\
\vspace{5mm}
\textbf{I do not accept SLOP}.

\part{Foundations}
\label{foundations}

% vision/motivation, what a Transputer is, the architecture, the ecosystem (IServer etc.)


Parachute is guided by the ideas of people who believed software could be lean, comprehensible, and purposeful:
\begin{itemize}
	\item The Inmos Transputer, occam, and C.A.R. Hoare's CSP — the technical foundation (with other languages)
	\item Niklaus Wirth's Project Oberon and his essay 'A Plea for Lean Software' — a philosophy of restraint
	\item The Amiga OS — elegance and capability in a small footprint
	\item Psion PDAs and the SIBO/EPOC operating systems — productive, focused, astonishingly efficient
	\item The Palm Pilot — proof that constraints produce clarity
\end{itemize}

Think: the early Mac, the Amiga, early Windows — but parallel, distributed, and built on a CPU that was genuinely ahead of its time.
	
And remember the PDA? Pocket-sized. Days of battery life on AAs. Distraction-free and productive. The smartphone was not its natural successor. Touch screens are necessary, but not sufficient for productive work - proper keyboards are essential.
